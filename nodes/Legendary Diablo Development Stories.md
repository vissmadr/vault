# Legendary Diablo Development Stories

Legendary stories from the insane development process of one of the best games of all time.

---

## Diablo II Was Almost a Fully 3D Game

Early in development, Blizzard North considered making Diablo II fully 3D.

However, late-1990s PC hardware created several problems:

- Hardware acceleration was inconsistent
- Performance dropped dramatically with many enemies on screen
- Large environments caused major framerate issues

Instead, the team used a hybrid approach:

- Characters and monsters were **3D models**
- These were **pre-rendered into 2D sprites**
- The game used an **isometric 2D engine**

This allowed:

- hundreds of enemies on screen
- very detailed animation frames
- consistent performance across PCs

Ironically, the compromise helped create the distinctive visual style that players still associate with Diablo II.

## The Brutal Development Crunch

Diablo II’s development cycle was chaotic and extremely demanding for the team.

The original planned release was **1999**, but the project fell behind schedule as features kept expanding.

By the final stretch:

- Developers were working **80–100 hour weeks**
- Some employees reportedly **slept at the office**
- Major systems like item balance and skills were still being adjusted **weeks before launch**

Despite the pressure, the team insisted on delivering ambitious features:

- **Five full acts**
- **Seven playable classes**
- Full online multiplayer through **Battle.net**

When Diablo II released in June 2000, it immediately became one of the fastest-selling PC games ever.

## The “Accidental Classic” Loot System

One of the defining features of Diablo II is its addictive loot system, but much of it emerged through experimentation rather than strict design.

During development at Blizzard North, designers kept modifying item generation systems based on internal playtests. They noticed that players reacted strongly whenever bosses dropped a large amount of gear at once.

Key decisions that shaped the system:

- Multiple rarity tiers were introduced over time: **Magic → Rare → Set → Unique**
- Item stats were intentionally **randomized within ranges**, creating endless variation
- Bosses were tuned to produce **large “loot explosions”** on death

A simple guiding philosophy emerged:

> When something dies, shower the player in treasure.

This approach became a core blueprint for later action RPGs like Path of Exile, Borderlands, and Diablo III.

## The Secret Cow Level (Born From a Community Joke)

The famous **Secret Cow Level** exists because of a rumor from the original Diablo.

Players believed that repeatedly clicking cows in the town area could open a secret cow-themed level. The rumor spread widely, even though it was completely false.

When developing Diablo II, the team decided to turn the joke into a real secret.

The final implementation:

- Combine **Wirt’s Leg** with a **Tome of Town Portal** in the **Horadric Cube**
- A portal opens to a hidden level filled entirely with **bipedal cows wielding polearms**
- The level contains a boss called the **Cow King**

In early versions of the game, killing the Cow King prevented reopening the portal, which created an extra layer of tension for players farming the area.

## Hardcore Mode Came From Developer Challenges

Hardcore mode — where death permanently deletes a character — was inspired by internal competitions among developers.

Team members would challenge each other to see who could play the longest without dying.

Eventually they formalized the idea into an official mode:

- If your character dies **once**, it is permanently dead
- The character cannot be revived or restored
- A tombstone appears in the character selection screen

Hardcore mode became one of the most intense ways to experience Diablo II and later influenced similar systems in many RPGs and roguelikes.

## Sound Design Was Built to Create Unease

The audio design of Diablo II was deliberately crafted to create constant tension.

Instead of loud music or heavy orchestration, composer **Matt Uelmen** created minimalist ambient tracks using acoustic guitar and sparse instrumentation.

The sound team also layered environmental audio such as:

- distant monster growls
- random dungeon noises
- echoes that changed depending on room size
- faint environmental ambience

The famous **Tristram theme** became one of the most recognizable pieces of music in gaming and helped define the game’s eerie atmosphere.

Players often reported feeling uneasy in dungeons even when no enemies were visible — exactly the effect the team intended.
